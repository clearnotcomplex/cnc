The Power of Subtraction

January 2025 • 8 min read

When faced with a problem, our instinct is almost always to add something. A new tool. A new process. A new feature. But some of the most elegant solutions come from removing things instead.

This is the power of subtraction—a principle that runs counter to our natural tendencies but often produces far better results.

Why We Default to Addition

Research by Gabrielle Adams and colleagues at the University of Virginia found that people consistently overlook subtractive changes when solving problems. In experiments where adding or removing elements were equally valid solutions, participants overwhelmingly chose addition.

There are several reasons for this bias. Addition feels like progress—something tangible we can point to. Subtraction, by contrast, can feel like giving up or admitting defeat. We're also often rewarded for what we create, not what we eliminate.

"Perfection is achieved, not when there is nothing more to add, but when there is nothing left to take away." — Antoine de Saint-Exupéry

Subtraction in Practice

The principle applies across domains:

In design: The best interfaces aren't those with the most features, but those that accomplish the task with the least friction. Apple's design philosophy under Jony Ive was famously about saying no to a thousand things.

In writing: Good editing is mostly about cutting. The first draft gets ideas down; subsequent drafts remove everything that doesn't earn its place.

In life: Often, eliminating a draining commitment does more for your wellbeing than adding a new self-improvement practice.

A Framework for Subtraction

When facing a problem, try asking these questions before reaching for addition:

  • What could I remove that would solve this?
  • What's adding complexity without proportional value?
  • If I were starting fresh, would I add this?
  • What would this look like if it were easy?

The Courage to Subtract

Subtraction requires confidence. It means trusting that less can be more, that removing a feature won't upset users, that declining a commitment won't damage a relationship.

But the evidence is clear: the most elegant solutions, the most enduring designs, the most focused lives—they're usually not the ones with the most things added. They're the ones where everything unnecessary has been stripped away.

The next time you face a problem, resist the urge to add. Ask instead: what can I subtract?
